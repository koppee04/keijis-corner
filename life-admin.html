★ LIFE ADMIN GUIDE ★
A practical playbook for young adults · Get your house in order

1
Financial
1
Build a clear picture of your money
Open a spreadsheet or use a budgeting app (YNAB, Monarch Money, or a Google Sheet). List every source of income and every recurring expense. Categorize spending into fixed (rent, insurance, subscriptions) and variable (food, entertainment, gas). Do this once, then review monthly.
2
Set up your accounts intentionally
You want at minimum: a checking account for daily spending, a high-yield savings account (HYSA) for your emergency fund, and a retirement account. If your employer offers a 401(k) with a match, contribute at least enough to get the full match — that's free money. Open a Roth IRA if you're eligible.
3
Build your emergency fund
Target 3–6 months of essential expenses. Keep this money in your HYSA, separate from your checking account. Automate a transfer to it each payday, even if it's a small amount.
4
Address debt strategically
List every debt with its balance, interest rate, and minimum payment. Prioritize high-interest debt (credit cards) using either the avalanche method (highest interest rate first) or snowball method (smallest balance first). Don't ignore low-interest debt, but don't panic about it either.
5
Monitor your credit
Check your credit report for free at AnnualCreditReport.com — you're entitled to a free report from each of the three bureaus yearly. Sign up for a free credit monitoring service. Dispute any errors in writing.
6
Set up for tax season before it arrives
Keep a folder for tax documents: W-2s, 1099s, receipts for deductible expenses, proof of student loan interest. If you have side income, track quarterly estimated tax payments. File by April 15 or request an extension.
2
Insurance
1
Inventory what you have
Write down every insurance policy you hold: health, dental, vision, auto, renter's/homeowner's. For each one, note the provider, monthly premium, deductible, and coverage limits. Keep policy numbers somewhere accessible.
2
Understand your health insurance
Know your deductible (what you pay before insurance kicks in), your out-of-pocket maximum (the most you'll pay in a year), and your copay/coinsurance. Know which doctors are in-network. Set a calendar reminder to review during open enrollment each fall.
3
Get renter's insurance if you rent
Renter's insurance typically costs $10–$20/month and covers your belongings against theft, fire, and water damage. It also includes personal liability coverage. Many providers (Lemonade, State Farm) let you set it up online in under 10 minutes.
4
Review coverage annually
Life changes — a new car, a new apartment, a new job — mean your insurance needs change too. Set a yearly reminder to review all policies. Compare quotes every couple of years; loyalty doesn't always pay with insurance companies.
3
Legal & Identity
1
Gather your critical documents
Locate and consolidate your: birth certificate, Social Security card, passport, professional licenses, vehicle titles, lease agreements, and insurance policies. Store originals in a fireproof box. Scan digital copies and store them in an encrypted folder or secure cloud storage.
2
Keep IDs current
Note expiration dates on your driver's license and passport. Set reminders 3 months before each expires — passport renewal can take 8–12 weeks. Many states allow online license renewals.
3
Designate beneficiaries
On every financial account that allows it (retirement accounts, life insurance, some bank accounts), designate a beneficiary. This overrides your will. Review these any time your life circumstances change significantly — marriage, divorce, death of a loved one.
4
Create a basic will or advance directive
A simple will can be created affordably through services like Trust & Will or Willing. At minimum, specify who gets your assets and who would make medical decisions for you if incapacitated (healthcare proxy or power of attorney).
5
Read contracts before you sign
Develop the habit of actually reading leases, employment agreements, and service contracts before signing. Flag anything unclear and ask questions. Keep a copy of every signed contract in your documents folder.
4
Health
1
Create a personal health record
Keep a simple document with your: blood type, known allergies, current medications and dosages, chronic conditions, immunization history, and past surgeries. Share it with your primary care doctor and keep a copy somewhere your emergency contacts can find it.
2
Schedule preventive appointments
Set recurring calendar reminders for annual physicals, dental cleanings (twice a year is better), and eye exams every 1–2 years. Many of these are covered fully under preventive care on most insurance plans.
3
Establish a primary care doctor
If you don't have one, find a PCP in your insurance network and make an introductory appointment. Having an established relationship with a doctor means you're not starting from scratch every time something comes up.
4
Update emergency contacts everywhere
Make sure emergency contacts are updated in your phone, at your workplace, with your doctor, and on relevant accounts. Confirm that a trusted person knows where your important documents are and can access them in an emergency.
5
Know your mental health resources
Identify at least one mental health resource before you need it — your insurance's mental health line, a therapist directory (Psychology Today, Open Path), or an employee assistance program. Knowing where to go is a meaningful step.
5
Career & Education
1
Keep your resume updated
After any significant project, certification, promotion, or new skill — update your resume. Keep a "brag document" too: a running log of accomplishments and wins you can draw from later. Don't wait until you're job hunting.
2
Understand your pay stub
Learn what FICA, federal/state withholding, and pre-tax deductions (health insurance, 401k contributions) mean. Use the IRS withholding calculator once a year to verify you're not dramatically over- or under-withholding.
3
Track licenses and certifications
As an electrician, your license renewal timeline is critical. Set reminders well in advance for every credential: state license, specialty endorsements, OSHA certifications, first aid/CPR. Losing a license through lapse is an avoidable disaster.
4
Know your full benefits package
Read through your employer's benefits guide once a year. Many people leave money on the table with HSA contributions, professional development stipends, or wellness reimbursements they didn't know existed.
6
Housing
1
Know your lease inside and out
When you sign or renew a lease, read it fully. Note: the exact rent amount, due date, notice required to move out, rules around subletting, and who is responsible for what maintenance. Keep a copy of the signed lease in your documents folder.
2
Track important dates
Set calendar reminders for: when your lease is up for renewal (usually 60 days' notice to move out), when renter's insurance renews, and any scheduled building inspections.
3
Document your space
When you move in, take dated photos or video of every room, noting any existing damage. This protects you against wrongful deductions from your security deposit. Send a copy to your landlord in writing so there's a timestamp.
4
Set up a maintenance routine
Set a seasonal reminder to: replace HVAC filters, test smoke and carbon monoxide detectors (and replace batteries), check for leaks under sinks, and clean dryer vents. Report any landlord-responsibility issues in writing, not just verbally.
5
Know your rights
California has some of the strongest tenant protections in the country, including limits on security deposit amounts and habitability requirements. Knowing these before a dispute arises puts you in a far better position.
7
Digital Life
1
Set up a password manager
Choose one password manager (Bitwarden is free and excellent; 1Password is great paid). Migrate your most important accounts first: email, banking, government, insurance. Use it to generate unique, strong passwords for each. This is one of the highest-leverage security moves you can make.
2
Enable two-factor authentication
Prioritize 2FA on: email, bank accounts, retirement accounts, your password manager, and any account tied to your identity. Use an authenticator app (Authy, Google Authenticator) rather than SMS when possible — it's meaningfully more secure.
3
Back up important data
Follow the 3-2-1 rule: 3 copies of important data, on 2 different media, with 1 offsite. In practice: files on your computer, backed up to an external hard drive, and synced to cloud storage (iCloud, Google Drive, Backblaze). Set this up once and automate it.
4
Audit your subscriptions
Go through your bank and credit card statements looking for recurring charges. Cancel anything you're not actively using. Use a tool like Rocket Money or a spreadsheet. People routinely discover they're paying for 3–5 things they forgot about.
5
Clean up your digital footprint periodically
Once a year: update email recovery options, review app permissions on your phone, check what apps have access to your Google or Apple account, and delete old accounts you no longer use. Tools like JustDeleteMe.xyz list how to delete accounts from hundreds of services.
8
Relationships & Estate
1
Make sure the right people know the right things
At minimum, one trusted person should know: where your important documents are, what financial accounts exist and how to access them, and who to contact (doctor, employer, bank) in an emergency. Write a one-page "in case of emergency" document and share it with this person.
2
Designate a healthcare proxy
A healthcare proxy (or healthcare power of attorney) is someone legally authorized to make medical decisions for you if you can't. This is a separate, simple document from a will. Choose someone who will honor your values and have a real conversation with them about what that means.
3
Understand digital account succession
Most major platforms (Google, Apple, Facebook) have a legacy contact or inactive account policy. Set these up so a trusted person can access or close your accounts if something happens to you.
4
Revisit everything after major life changes
Marriage, having children, buying a home, changing jobs, losing someone close — these are triggers to revisit beneficiaries, insurance coverage, your will, and emergency contacts. Build this review into any major life transition.

Getting Started: A Suggested Order
Week 1Create a master document folder (digital and/or physical). Locate birth certificate, SS card, and passport.
Week 2Set up or audit your budgeting system. Know your monthly income and expenses.
Week 3Check your insurance coverage. Get renter's insurance if you don't have it.
Week 4Set up a password manager and enable 2FA on your most critical accounts.
Month 2Schedule all overdue health appointments.
Month 3Draft a simple will or advance directive. Designate beneficiaries on all accounts.
OngoingSet a recurring calendar event once a year: "Life Admin Review." Use it to check expiration dates, review coverage, audit subscriptions, and update documents.
The goal isn't perfection. It's building systems so that life's inevitable surprises don't become emergencies.
