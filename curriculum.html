// How to use this curriculum

This curriculum is designed for self-directed adult study at roughly one hour per day, seven days a week — approximately 30 hours per month. The full arc from Phase 1 through Phase 5 spans about three years. The pace is deliberate: science and mathematics cannot be absorbed by reading alone. Every week, work problems.

The Fundamental Rule

Do not move to the next topic until you can solve problems from the current one without looking at examples. Reading a chapter and understanding it feels like progress; working through a problem set and getting stuck is actual progress. The sticking points are where learning happens.

Weekly Rhythm

Days 1–2: Learn the new concept (video or textbook). Understand it at a high level before doing anything else.
Days 3–5: Work problems. Start with easier ones and increase difficulty.
Days 6–7: Review the week’s material. Rework problems from scratch, not by reading the solution.

When You Get Stuck

Sit with confusion for at least 20 minutes before seeking help. Looking up the answer immediately prevents the struggle that cements understanding. After 20 minutes, check your textbook, then Khan Academy, then seek an explanation. Once you understand it, close the source and redo the problem yourself.

Crossing Between Phases

Do not skip the Phase review weeks. They are not optional. Each one is a genuine checkpoint: work through a set of mixed problems from the phase, identify weak areas, and return to them before continuing. The curriculum is sequential — gaps in foundations cause compounding difficulties later.

Philosophy of Science Thread

Begin Kuhn’s Structure of Scientific Revolutions during Phase 1 — it will reframe how you think about what you are doing from the start. Return to Popper and Deutsch later, once you have the scientific content they are describing.

On Mathematics

Mathematics is not a spectator sport. The difference between someone who “understands” calculus and someone who has learned it is measured entirely in problems worked. Aim for at least 20 to 30 problems per topic before moving on.

Phase 1 — ~26 weeks (~6 months)

Mathematical Foundations

Pre-algebra & Algebra (Wks 1–10)  •  Geometry & Trigonometry (Wks 11–18)  •  Precalculus (Wks 19–26)

Mathematics is the language the natural world is written in. Everything in Phases 2 through 5 depends on this foundation. Starting from rusty basics, this phase moves through algebra, geometry, trigonometry, and precalculus. Take the time this phase requires — a solid algebraic instinct saves enormous pain later.

By the end of Week 26, you should be able to graph any polynomial or trigonometric function, solve a system of equations, work with logarithms and exponents fluently, and describe what a limit means informally. If any of these feel shaky, invest another two weeks in review before entering Phase 2.

Phase 2 — ~24 weeks (~6 months)

Calculus & Classical Physics

Single-Variable Calculus (Wks 1–14)  •  Classical Mechanics (Wks 15–24)

This is the phase where mathematics becomes physics. Calculus is the language of change — derivatives describe rates, integrals describe accumulation. Classical mechanics applies these tools to motion, force, and energy. This is Newton’s universe, and it is extraordinarily beautiful to see it from the inside.

By Week 24, you should be able to differentiate and integrate standard functions, explain what a derivative means geometrically, draw a free body diagram and apply Newton’s laws, and derive the equations of projectile motion from first principles. The capstone goal: understand why planetary orbits are ellipses, derived from calculus and Newton’s law of gravity.

Phase 3 — ~30 weeks (~7 months)

Mathematical Tools & Chemistry

Linear Algebra (Wks 1–8)  •  Statistics & Probability (Wks 9–15)  •  General Chemistry (Wks 16–27)  •  Multivariable Calculus (Wks 28–30)

This phase expands the mathematical toolkit and introduces the science that bridges physics and biology. Linear algebra reveals the geometric structure underlying quantum mechanics and data. Statistics teaches you to read evidence critically — essential for anyone engaging with science seriously. Chemistry explains how the atomic world produces the molecular world, which produces life.

By Week 30, you should understand Bayes’ theorem and why it matters for reasoning, be able to multiply matrices and find eigenvalues, balance a chemical equation and use the ideal gas law, and have a working understanding of thermodynamics and entropy. At this point, you are equipped to read most scientific papers and follow their reasoning.

Phase 4 — ~26 weeks (~7 months)

Deeper Physics & Life Sciences

Electricity, Magnetism & Waves (Wks 1–12)  •  Molecular & Cell Biology (Wks 13–26)

Physics deepens into electricity, magnetism, and waves — culminating in Maxwell’s equations and the electromagnetic nature of light. Biology is approached at its most rigorous level: the molecular machinery of life. By the end of this phase, you will understand how physics produces chemistry, and how chemistry produces biology. The connections between these levels are among the most astonishing in all of science.

By Week 26, you should understand how Maxwell’s equations predict light, follow the full path from DNA to protein to cell function, and be able to describe photosynthesis and cellular respiration at the molecular level. At this point you have the foundation to read primary research literature in physics and biology — begin supplementing with journal papers.

Phase 5 — ~25 weeks + ongoing

Synthesis & Frontier Questions

Modern Physics (Wks 1–12)  •  Evolution & Complexity (Wks 13–20)  •  Philosophy of Science (Wks 21–25 and ongoing)

Phase 5 is where the threads converge. Modern physics reveals that the world at small scales and high velocities is nothing like everyday intuition. Evolution explains the staggering complexity of life as the product of blind, cumulative processes. Philosophy of science examines what the scientific enterprise actually is, what it can and cannot tell us, and how it relates to the deepest questions humans have asked.

Phase 5 has no natural terminus. The curriculum gives you a structured entry into each of these domains, but the questions themselves — about the nature of reality, the foundations of mathematics, the origin of life, the relationship between science and meaning — are open. By Week 25, you will have the tools to continue engaging them seriously for the rest of your life.

The physicist Eugene Wigner wrote in 1960 about “the unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics in the natural sciences” — the mysterious fact that abstract mathematical structures invented by pure mathematicians for their own reasons turn out, again and again, to describe physical reality with uncanny precision. Nobody knows why this is.

You are beginning a journey toward the place where that mystery is most acute. The further you go in this curriculum, the more clearly you will see how the questions at the frontier of science — about space, time, consciousness, life, and the nature of mathematical truth — are not separate from the questions you have been asking all along in philosophy and theology. They are the same questions, approached from a different direction.

The curriculum gives you the tools. The questions were always already yours.
